Active Learning in Higher Education

A conceptual introduction through guided reading of course literature

Session approach
What is guided reading?
▼

This session used guided reading as its primary method. Rather than presenting a lecture about active learning, the instructor read selectively from the course literature alongside participants, pausing at key moments to prompt note-taking, small group discussion, and reflection. The aim was to build a shared conceptual understanding of core terms before exploring specific strategies in later sessions.

Instructor — session opening
"The purpose here is that we collect information from this and the articles, so that later on in the session today we can actually gather it and perhaps even make notes together."

The session worked through two core texts: the introductory chapter from Active Learning Strategies in Higher Education and Chapter 2 by Stetson-Tiligadas, "Designing for Active Learning: A Problem-Centered Approach." The Cambridge Assessment brief on active learning was used as a secondary anchor text.

"This chapter concerns itself primarily with questions of how students in higher education studies can best acquire, apply, create, and share knowledge."
Active Learning Strategies in Higher Education — chapter opening, read aloud during session
"As instructors in higher education, our teaching is grounded in our conceptions of how people learn. We must be mindful that, for most of our students, the academy will not be their career destination. Our students will move into the world of service and the world of work, where they will need to know how to function well."

TheoryPiaget / Vygotsky

Constructivism & social constructivism

"Active learning is based on a theory of learning called constructivism, which emphasises the fact that learners construct or build their understanding. Jean Piaget (1896–1980), a psychologist and founder of constructivism, researched the cognitive development of children, observing that their knowledge was individually built up, bit by bit. In the process of making meaning, children replace or adapt their existing knowledge and understanding with deeper levels of understanding."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
"The theory of social constructivism says that learning happens primarily through social interaction with others, such as a teacher or a learner's peers."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
Session discussion
The instructor used nursing as an example: "Can I become a nurse by myself? It's quite difficult actually." This grounded the distinction between individual and social constructivism in a familiar professional context.
TheoryVygotsky

Zone of Proximal Development

"Lev Vygotsky (1896–1934) described the zone of proximal development (ZPD). This is the area where learning activities should be focused, lying between what the learner can achieve independently and what the learner can achieve with expert guidance."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
Session framing
"There is something that the instructor can do — that the teacher needs to know about the participant or the student — that you have to know where to start and where to go, what they know and what they don't know. And the zone of proximal development is the concept that that's where you want to focus the energy of teaching and learning."
Practical implication raised in session
"I need to start my course with a diagnostic." Knowing where students are when they arrive has very real consequences for planning and pacing.
TheoryBruner (not Vygotsky)

Scaffolding

"Scaffolding describes the support a student or group of students receive as they work towards a learning goal. The idea of scaffolding was developed by cognitive psychologist Jerome Bruner (1915–2016) researching oral language acquisition in children. Grounded in social constructivism, the process of scaffolding 'enables a child or novice to solve a problem, carry out a task or achieve a goal which would be beyond his unassisted efforts' (Wood, Bruner & Ross, 1976, p.90). Scaffolding learning is important to secure new knowledge and/or skills but equally, support should be withdrawn over time and when appropriate, to allow students to develop independence."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
Correction made in session
The instructor noted that scaffolding is often incorrectly attributed to Vygotsky: "it wasn't actually Vygotsky's idea, but Bruner rather."
Participant definitions generated during session
"Scaffolding is a process — when a more experienced person helps another person to master a skill." / "My understanding of scaffolding is providing assistance to develop learning." / "To give support to help someone at the beginning until the person becomes independent."
Instructor synthesis
"The best scaffolding — the student always feels independent, but the scaffolding is just the little thing they need... they feel that they are doing it themselves. Perhaps a key word here is also autonomy."
ConceptCambridge Assessment

Sense-making

"For learners to make sense of new information and ideas, they need to make links with existing knowledge, so that they can process and then understand new material. This sense-making is an active process which can take place during a wide range of learning activities. It can be contrasted with a passive approach to learning in which the teacher primarily talks 'at' students and simply assumes they will make sense of what is said without needing to check."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
Instructor prompt during session
"In your notes, write down: what is sense-making? How do I make that happen? And the next question is: how do I design a learning activity that will help the students understand — the function of the learning activity — make sense of things. If I can create that, then that ought to be active learning."
ConceptPellegrino / Hattie

Metacognition

"Active learning requires students to think hard and to practise using new knowledge and skills in order to develop long-term recall and a deeper understanding."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
Instructor reflection
"There's a big difference between learning how to learn a subject. I think that very many academics overlook how difficult it is to learn how to learn the subject. And a student not knowing how to learn a subject, becoming reliant on the teacher trying to soak up all of the teacher's knowledge — that's a very big difference."
TheoryStetson-Tiligadas / Sweller

Cognitive load theory

"Cognitive load is considered 'the major factor in determining the success of instructional intervention in attaining transfer of knowledge and skills' (Paas et al., 2005, p. 26). Cognitive load theory points to two types of load on working memory: intrinsic load and extraneous load, and as Sweller (1994) noted, any information that leads to high cognitive load, no matter which type, constitutes a significant challenge to effective learning."
Stetson-Tiligadas (2018) — Designing for Active Learning, Ch. 2
"Extraneous cognitive load is the load placed on working memory, not by the learning content itself, but by instructional methods, activities, and presentation. Sweller (1994) further remarked that 'extraneous cognitive load, by definition, is entirely under instructional control'."
Stetson-Tiligadas (2018)
Session application
If a teacher begins too high up in Bloom's pyramid without establishing the base, "it'll be whoosh — right over their heads." The connection between cognitive load and appropriate sequencing was used to motivate both Bloom's taxonomy and the diagnostic function of ZPD.

Teacher roles
Activator vs. facilitator
▼

One of the most discussed misconceptions about active learning is that it means stepping back. The session addressed this through Hattie's research and the activator/facilitator distinction.

Teacher as activator

  • Direct instruction and modelling
  • Teaching metacognition explicitly
  • Connecting to prior knowledge
  • Diagnosing misconceptions
Effect size: 0.60 (Hattie, 2009)

Teacher as facilitator

  • Simulations, gaming, discovery
  • Students largely self-directed
  • Minimal instructor intervention
  • Problem-based learning contexts
Effect size: 0.17 (Hattie, 2009)
"Hattie (2009, p.243) distinguishes between the teacher as activator and the teacher as facilitator. As an activator, teachers are key agents in leading strategies which will include direct instruction and teaching metacognition. In contrast, a facilitator role could work better in strategies such as simulations and gaming, and problem-based learning. He reports that the effect size is greater when the teacher acts as an activator (0.60 compared to 0.17 for facilitator)."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
"Misconceptions about active learning strategies. Active learning includes a wide range of teaching and learning strategies which do not necessarily involve learners moving around the room or undertaking group work. Active learning is happening if students are thinking hard and relating their new learning to existing ideas in a way that enables them to make progress."
Cambridge Assessment — Active Learning brief
On visible learning and formative assessment
"The idea of gaining information about students' misconceptions, perhaps even foreseeing students' misconceptions, or making them visual — accessible to the instructor — is one of the key points. Sometimes we call it visible learning."
Discussion prompt used in session
"How will I, the teacher, the instructor, know whether or not you have understood or not?"
Strategies
Problem-based vs. problem-centered learning
▼
"True problem-based learning presupposes highly trained tutors and a significant investment into facilities and support materials so that a small group of learners, no more than seven, have access to resources as and when they deem appropriate in the course of solving the central problem."
Stetson-Tiligadas (2018), citing Barrows (2002)
"Problem-centered learning does not require a rigid process and can be used to describe learning environments where knowledge and skills are acquired through solving authentic problems with appropriate support and scaffolding used when necessary."
Stetson-Tiligadas (2018)
"Learning is promoted when: (1) learners work with authentic problems, (2) new knowledge is introduced after prior knowledge has been activated, (3) new knowledge is demonstrated to the learner (rather than simply being told to the learner), (4) the learner can practice applying the new knowledge, and (5) there is integration of the new knowledge into the learner's context."
Merrill (2002) — First Principles of Instruction, as summarised in Stetson-Tiligadas
Session discussion — practical reality
A participant raised the challenge of motivation in larger groups: "If we have a discussion forum, not all students enter into the discussion forum. So how do we make people really wanting to engage and wanting to be active?"
Instructor framing for the rest of the course
"As we move on through the course, we're going to use our understanding of the concept, and then we're going to go on a hunt for specific strategies like these, that work in our context. Because maybe problem-based learning with a small group of seven perhaps isn't the optimal choice for everybody."
